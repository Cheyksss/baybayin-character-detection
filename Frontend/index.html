Baybayin Reference Guide

The modern Baybayin syllabary has 17 base characters and kudlit (diacritical) marks that modify vowel sounds. Use this guide to cross-check detection results and learn to read the script yourself.

i

Baybayin has three independent vowel characters. When a vowel starts a syllable or stands alone, these characters are used directly.

c

Consonant characters inherently carry an "A" vowel sound. A kudlit mark above adds an "I/E" sound; below adds "O/U". A cross kudlit (virama) cancels the vowel entirely.

Understanding Kudlit (ᜌ Diacritical Marks)

Kudlits are small marks placed above or below a character to modify its vowel sound. The system classifies all 59 combinations — 17 base characters × 3 vowel states + virama.

1

Writing Direction

Traditionally, Baybayin was written from left to right, top to bottom — the same direction as modern Filipino. Characters representing syllables are stacked horizontally.

2

Syllabic Nature

Each character represents a consonant-vowel syllable (CV), not individual letters. The character for "BA" is one mark — not two separate letters B and A.

3

Kudlit System

Place a dot or tick above a consonant to change its vowel to "I/E". Place it below to change it to "O/U". A cross-mark (virama) removes the vowel sound entirely.

4

Word Boundaries

In classical Baybayin, words were often written continuously with spaces separating them. Final consonants at the end of a word are sometimes dropped unless a virama is used.
